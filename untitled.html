1. Introduction
Biorefinery is an integrated processing facility where biomass is converted into a variety of products ranging from fuels to value-added chemicals after many physical, chemical and thermal conversion technologies, with minimal waste and emissions. In a biorefinery, variety of feedstocks, many products and large number of technologies exist, and this brings great challenges to the process developers while considering economic performance, energy requirement and environmental impact simultaneously. A systematic approach, for the design of biorefinery, integrates the use of advanced process synthesis, process analysis and optimization methods (Antonis 2012). Development of such comprehensive methods is very crucial to develop, design and commercialize sustainable and cost-effective biorefineries (Yuan 2013). Process synthesis methods collect the input data about the biorefinery processes and produce energetically and economically efficient process flowsheets with good operating conditions and design specifications. Process modelling and simulation are complementary approaches to analyse the different designs obtained from process synthesis for the optimal configurations of biorefineries (Martín 2012 and Metzger 2012).  Over the last 45 years, researchers have studied process synthesis area, and several researchers have published extensive reviews on this subject (Hendry 1973, Hlaváček 1978, Westerberg 1980, Stephanopoulos 1981 and Nishida 1981). Recently, several researchers have been working in the field of process synthesis and design of large scale biorefinery systems. For example, the studies in Baliban 2013 and Tay 2011 addressed the determination of the single best technology for the production of single products, especially bio-fuels. Further, there are also some studies that take into account superstructures with multiple feedstocks, products and conversion pathways (Murillo-Alvarado 2013, Santiban~ez-Aguilar 2011 and Kim 2013).
In the next sections, a brief review of advances in the area of process system engineering is provided, where available systematic techniques for process design, integration, and optimization are introduced.

2. Flowsheeting Approaches in Process System Engineering
The process flowsheeting approaches are generalized into three categories or some combination thereof: (1) methods that use heuristics, (2) methods that use thermodynamic targets and process integration, and (3) methods that use superstructures, mathematical programming and optimization. All these three approaches focus on flowsheet synthesis that results in economics optimization. In hierarchical approach, one can decide whether the plant will be operated in batch or continuous, type of reactor used, recycle schemes for material, methods and sequence of seperations, energy integration applied, etc. In thermodynamic analysis, one has to decide the units and streams which are included in the pinch analysis, thermodynamic targets used and the level of utilities are involved. In optimization approach, extent of the superstructure, included physical data, employed objective function, constraints and uncertainties to consider, are the features to be decided by engineer (Seferlis 2004). Major contributions in the first two approaches (heuristics and thermodynamic targets) are hierarchical decomposition (Douglas 1988) and pinch analysis (Linnhoff 1993), which have been successfully applied in many industrial applications (Martín 2012). More recent trend is combining mathematical programming approach with algorithmic methods (or optimization techniques) which can be used effectively in process synthesis (Grossmann 1999). There are three steps in the combined approach: (1) the development of a representation of alternative superstructures, (2) the formulation of a mathematical program for the selection of the configuration and operating levels from the superstructure, and (3) the solution of the optimization model (Martín 2012).

3. Modelling Approaches in Process Design
In the context of process synthesis, process models are necessary for simulating process flowsheets. Mathematical model is used in process simulation that represents the behavior of the process. A mathematical process model consists a set of variables which describes important properties of the process and a set of equations that builds relationships among variables to explain the behavior of the process. For complex systems found in chemical industry, the process models represent mostly nonlinear behaviors. With the increased interest in process synthesis, the demand for process models with increased accuracy is also growing. One important concept in process system engineering is superstructure optimization. A superstructure contains most (or all) of the system alternatives. Superstructures are defined by the process modeller, and alternative superstructures can be derived for the same process. Then, mathematical optimization approach seeks to find the optimal configuration among the proposed alternatives (Trespalacios 2014).

4. Process Integration
Process integration is a design approach that deals with the energy efficiency, waste minimization and an efficient use of raw materials. Process integration stage is greatly facilitated by the use of process simulations. In biorefineries, process integration plays an important role to study strong trade offs (Antonis 2012).

4.1. Heat Integration
To create a heat cascade in processes, pinch analysis is a very powerful thermodynamics based technique with a structured approach to identify minimum energy consumption targets for heating and cooling, and maximum internal heat recovery within a process (Linnhoff 1993, Ray 1995 and El-Halwagi 2008). This concept is also applicable in other areas in addition to heat recovery. In fact, whenever an amount (e.g., heat or mass) has a quality (e.g., temperature or concentration), the concept of composite curves can provide a view of the problem related to efficient recovery (or re-use) of resources. On the composite curves, pinch point shows the location where there is an accumulated deficit of an amount above a certain quality (Gundersen 2000). Marechal and Kalitventzeff (Maréchal 1998) developed process integration techniques in their studies. Duran and Grossmann, 1986 Duran 1986 proposed an algorithm for simultaneous heat integration and optimization of chemical processes. Pfeffer et al., 2007 Pfeffer 2007 applied process integration into a bioethanol production plant with an optimization objective of minimization of heat demand. More recently, Gassner and Marechal, 2009 Gassner 2009 developed a methodology that can be applied for the conceptual design of bio fuel plants based on optimization with identification of promising flow-sheets and process integration methods. They applied process synthesis on wood gasification processes.

4.2. Mass integration
Mass integration is a systematic methodology to the generation, separation, and allocation of streams and species along the process. Mass pinch is developed by El-Halwagi and Manousiouthakis (1989-1990) El-Halwagi 1989, El-Halwagi 1990 and it is applicable into industrial processes where mass exchange occurs between process streams in a number of process units such as extractors, absorbers. They considered the transfer of only a single contaminant from a rich stream to a lean stream where a rich stream has a higher concentration of contaminant while a lean stream represents a stream with a lower concentration of the contaminant. The target in this study was to minimize fresh water flow rate. Mass pinch can be applied in the area of wastewater minimization where the water and wastewater is optimally used by reuse, regeneration and recycling. Wang and Smith, 1994 Wang 1994 aimed at minimizing wastewater based on maximum water re-use and they defined the water pinch concentration and targeted the minimum freshwater consumption.

5. Costing and Life Cycle Analysis
With the growing interest in sustainability, biorefineries are likely to play significant roles in enhancing the energy security and mitigating the climate changes. Process modeling and simulation are essential for predicting the economic, environomic and social performance of industrial processes. In the recent past, researchers are mainly focusing on the development of cost-effective biorefineries that can only be achieved by optimal mass and energy integrations. In order to make the bio-refinery profitable, production of value added products (e.g., succinic acid, dimethyl ether, etc.), valorization of waste mass and energy streams, and cogeneration are significantly important. If biorefinery is only producing biofuels, environmental incentives may be required. The sustainable growth of biorefinery not only requires cost-effective products, but also energy efficient plants. The environomic assessment of biorefinery includes land use change, greenhouse gas emission, timing of emission, waste production, environmental impact of product, etc. Some studies in literature are specifically focusing on the life-cycle assessment of biorefineries (Ahlgren et al., 2015).
Biorefinery can use many traditional equipment from the petrochemical industry (e.g., distillation column, pump, heat exchange, compressors, etc.), and cost functions for these equipment are well established (Turton et al., 2009). Conversely, some of the biorefinery processes are underdevelopment (e.g., gasifier, fuel cell, membrane separation, etc.), so their future costs are highly uncertain (Caliandro, 2014). Generally, production cost goes down with an increase in the plant size, and optimal plant capacity depends on economic value of product. Since biomass is diluted and diversified in vast area, so supply chain optimization and economic viability study for different sizes of biorefineries is also critical for investment planning.  

6. Optimization
The development of novel products from biorefineries usually focus the conversion of biomass, selection of reaction pathway and maximum reaction yield. After that, different conversion and separation steps are included to form a biorefinery superstructure. The biorefinery superstructure can be optimized for different performance objectives such as total cost and carbon dioxide emissions (Celebi et al., 2016). The optimization algorithm may generate different biorefinery configurations via product and technology selection, energy and mass integration, depending on the objective of interest, raw material and utility costs, and product price. Gebreslassie et al. (2012) have considered multi-objective and multi-period optimization of biorefinery supply chains under supply and demand uncertainties. Geraili et al (2015) used multi-objective evolutionary algorithm to quantify the tradeoffs between cost and financial risk for biorefineries. Stuart and El-Halwagi (2012) examined the integration of biorefineries into existing processes and infrastructure.
